Introduction

Jose rizal, The national hero of the Philippines, was not only a doctor and a patriot but also an exceptional writer whose works ignited the flames of revolution. His poe, Mi Ultimo Adios is a heartfelt farewell to his beloved country, expressing deep love and patriotism in the face of impending death. Written on the eve of his execution, the poem is a powerful testament to his unwavering commitment to Philippines independence. The verses resonate with profound emotions grief, hope, and sacrifice. Rizal's words continue to inspire generations, reminding us of the power of literature in shaping national identity.

Rizal was polymath, mastering multiple languages, including Spanish, French, German, and English among others, His intelligence was matched by his deep compassion for his countrymen, as seen in his extensive travel and studies abroad. Throughout his life, he sought ways to reform the oppressive Spansh rule without resorting to violence. His novels, Noli me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, exposed the corruption of the Spanish clergy and government, awakening Filipino consciousness to the injustice they Suffered.

Mi Último Adiós serves as an enduring symbol of Rizal’s sacrifices. It was hidden inside an oil lamp, discovered only after his execution. The poem’s themes—love for the homeland, acceptance of fate, and hope for a better future—reflect Rizal’s beliefs that the youth must continue the fight for freedom. He did not advocate for war, but rather for intellectual awakening and unity. His execution on December 30, 1896, solidified his role as the catalyst for the Philippine Revolution. Today, his legacy lives on in history books, monuments, and the minds of Filipinos who honor his contributions.

Biography

José Protacio Rizal Mercado y Alonzo Realonda was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines. He was the seventh child of Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso, both of whom were well-educated and financially stable. Rizal showed early signs of brilliance, mastering the alphabet by the age of three and writing poetry as a young boy. His parents nurtured his intellect, enrolling him in the Ateneo Municipal de Manila, where he excelled in academics.

Rizal’s academic journey began in Biñan, where he was taught by private tutors before attending the Ateneo Municipal de Manila. He graduated with high honors and continued his education at the University of Santo Tomas, initially studying philosophy before shifting to medicine. His desire to help his blind mother motivated him to specialize in ophthalmology. However, faced with discrimination in his own country, he decided to pursue higher studies abroad.

Rizal’s literary works, especially Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, were instrumental in exposing Spanish colonial abuses. His writings inspired Filipinos to seek independence, alarming the Spanish authorities, who branded him a subversive. He was exiled to Dapitan, where he continued his humanitarian efforts—establishing a school, providing free medical care, and conducting scientific research.

Despite his nonviolent stance, Rizal was arrested, accused of inciting rebellion, and sentenced to death. On December 30, 1896, he was executed at Bagumbayan (now Luneta Park). His final act—turning to face the firing squad—symbolized his courage and dignity. Today, José Rizal is remembered not just as a martyr but as a visionary whose ideals continue to inspire a nation.

On December 30, 1896, at the age of 35, José Rizal was executed by a firing squad in Bagumbayan (now Luneta Park). His final poem, Mi Último Adiós, written before his execution, expressed his deep love for his country and willingness to die for its freedom. Rizal’s sacrifice cemented his role as the Philippines' national hero. His legacy lives on in history books, monuments, and the ideals he fought for—justice, education, and national identity. Today, his contributions continue to inspire Filipinos to strive for a better future.
